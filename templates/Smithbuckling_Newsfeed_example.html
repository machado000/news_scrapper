Weinstein Group Revises Sculptor Bid After Board’s Concerns
Bloomberg (09/06/23) Burton, Katherine

Boaz Weinstein and his group of bidders have reportedly revised their offer to purchase Sculptor Capital Management Inc. (SCU), seeking to address some of the concerns the board’s special committee outlined in a proxy filing last week. While Weinstein’s bid remains at $12.76 a share, it has beefed up its equity commitments, eliminated risks associated with debt financing and increased the damages it would pay if it fails to consummate the transaction, people familiar with the matter said. Sculptor accepted Rithm Capital Corp.’s lower bid of $11.15 a share, or about $639 million, in July. It still preferred that offer as of a filing on Aug. 30, citing a variety of concerns about the higher bid from the other group. It is unclear whether Sculptor will be swayed by the amended proposal. The group, which also includes Bill Ackman, Marc Lasry, and Jeff Yass, increased its offer last week by 51 cents a share. Former Sculptor Chief Executive Officer Rob Shafir, who owns 6.2% of the firm’s Class A common stock, later issued a statement calling it “clearly superior” to the Rithm deal. The higher offer is also favored by Dan Och, who founded the firm previously known as Och-Ziff, and four other former executives. Sculptor, led by Chief Executive Officer Jimmy Levin, has said Bidder J’s proposal is less attractive in part because of the risk that clients won’t accept that buyer’s choice to replace him. While Rithm plans to retain Levin, Weinstein’s group has said it would oust him as chief investment officer, according to a proxy filing.

Orange Capital to Vote FOR the Merger and Internalization Transaction at the GNL Special Meeting
PRNewswire (09/06/23)

Orange Capital Ventures on Wednesday announced its intention to vote in favor of the proposed internalization and merger transaction after reaching out to management, performing a thorough review of Global Net Lease, Inc.'s (GNL) presentation filed on August 28, and its related joint proxy materials, participating in the investor call hosted on September 5, and entering into a confidential agreement with the company's advisor and the company following discussions with its management. Pursuant to the agreement, the advisor, at no expense to the company, will reimburse Orange Capital for its time and expenses depending on the outcome of the vote. Orange Capital agrees that the proposed internalization consideration is within the range of precedent transactions as determined by BMO given the size and scope of the proposed transaction and compared to comparable market transactions. Under the agreement, Orange Capital has entered into a customary standstill agreement and will vote in favor of the company's directors at the 2024 annual meeting.

GameStop Stock Jumps After Results Top Estimates, Helped by International Gains
MarketWatch (09/06/23) Peters, Bill

Shares of GameStop Corp. (GME) rose in extended trading Wednesday after the videogame retailer reported second-quarter results that beat expectations, lifted by international sales gains and what the company described as “a significant software release.” The company — which over the past few months has made some big leadership changes — reported a net loss of $2.8 million, or 1 cent a share, compared with $108.7 million, or 36 cents a share, in the same quarter last year. Revenue crept higher to $1.16 billion, compared with $1.14 billion in the prior-year quarter. GameStop’s adjusted net loss per share was 3 cents. The company ended the quarter with cash and equivalents of $1.195 billion, compared with around $1.3 billion in the prior quarter. Analysts polled by FactSet expected a 14-cent adjusted per-share loss, on revenue of $1.14 billion. Shares rose 3.6% after hours. The company, as with its previous round of earnings, said it would not be holding a conference call. GameStop reported its results in the wake of the firing of its chief executive and the election of investor Ryan Cohen to executive chair in June, followed by the resignation of its chief financial officer. Cohen over the past few years has amassed greater influence over the company. Wall Street, however, has had broader concerns about a shift to mobile and digital gaming, and the threat posed to physical videogame retailers.

Bank of America Most Favored for Activism Defense in First Half of 2023, LSEG Data Show
Reuters (09/06/23) Herbst-Bayliss, Svea

Data from LSEG shows Bank of America (BAC) rose to become the top financial adviser to companies engaged by activist investors in the first half of this year. The bank advised on 33 campaigns, including defending Salesforce (CRM) against Elliott Investment Management, Starboard Value, Inclusive Capital Partners, and ValueAct. It also joined LivePerson (LPSN) in its fight against Starboard and Life Storage when Public Storage (PSA) made an unsolicited bid. Second-place financial adviser Goldman Sachs (GS) worked on 30 campaigns ahead of third-place-ranked JPMorgan Chase (JPM), which participated in 23 campaigns. Bank of America came in fifth place with 12 mandates, lagging behind Goldman, JPMorgan, Spotlight Advisers, and Morgan Stanley (MS). Sidley Austin maintained its position as the activism-focused law firm most favored by corporations with 32 mandates. The busiest law firm was Olshan Frome Wolosky, which works exclusively with activists, with 44 campaigns. Sidley overtook Vinson & Elkins LLP, which had 23 campaigns, and Schulte Roth & Zabel, which also works solely with activists, with 22 mandates. Wilson Sonsini rose from seventh to fifth place with 15 campaigns. The top proxy solicitor, with 106 campaigns, continued to be Innisfree M&A. The eight leading advisers, including Spotlight, LDG Advisory, Lazard, and Evercore Partners, participated in 136 mandates in the first half of 2023, down from 165 a year ago. Various providers collate league tables that are often used to garner new clients, yet bankers say discrepancies can crop up because many companies ward off activists privately and request advisers to keep quiet about their role.

European Shareholders Contest More Executive Pay Reports
Reuters (09/06/23) Wilkes, Tommy

European shareholders are increasingly contesting corporate executive pay reports, data showed on Wednesday, as investors unhappy with companies' approach to environmental and social issues vote against director remuneration. The percentage of remuneration reports disputed by investors across seven countries reached 42.9% during the 2023 annual general meeting season, the highest in at least five years, according to an annual review by shareholder engagement firm Georgeson. Daniele Vitale, Georgeson's head of governance UK & Europe, said limited environmental and social resolutions meant shareholders were using pay reports to voice dissent. Georgeson defines a vote as contested if at least 10% of votes are against. "There is also a perception that some companies have used ESG metrics in pay to make it easier for executives to meet payouts," said Vitale. Switzerland saw the highest proportion of contested resolutions on remuneration reporting, at 68.4%. The UK had the least, at 20.2%, but its percentage has been rising and is up from 16.2% in 2021, Georgeson found. Opposition to executive pay looks likely to increase too, as big asset managers including BlackRock (BLK) and State Street adopt so-called devolved proxy voting rights to end investors, who may vote differently from institutional shareholders, said Domenic Brancati, Georgeson's global chief operating officer. Georgeson found that the percentage of disputed resolutions on remuneration policy dropped to 29.2% from 34.8% in 2022. The proportion of contested director election resolutions reached 11.7% from 11.2% in 2022, the review showed. European shareholders remain reluctant to vote against directors because it is considered more confrontational, Vitale said, but he expects that will change as investors' environmental and social concerns grow.

GOP ESG Bills Await U.S. House Floor Consideration
National Law Review (09/06/23) Crowley, Daniel F.C.; Page, Karishma Shah; Heiman, Bruce J.

Patrick McHenry (R-NC), chair of the House Financial Services Committee (HFSC), recently concluded a month-long series of hearings regarding digital assets and ESG legislation. During markups held on July 26-27, the HFSC advanced several bills on these issues, both on a bipartisan basis (digital assets and stablecoin) and along party lines (anti-ESG bills). Prior to the ESG markup, GOP members of the HFSC unveiled 18 bills that would be under consideration, but the measures were subsequently bundled into larger packages that likely would be solely backed by Republicans. In the near future, McHenry is expected to seek full House consideration of the committee's bills. Meanwhile, the leading congressional priorities are consideration of the fiscal year (FY) 2024 appropriations bills, the FY2024 National Defense Authorization Act, the Federal Aviation Administration reauthorization, and the Farm Bill. However, the GOP-led House may consider and pass the HFSC's bills related to ESG and digital assets. It is also possible there will be room for bipartisan agreement on some of these issues going forward. The congressional focus on ESG will likely be impacted by the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission's (SEC) long-awaited final rule on climate risk disclosures and proposed rule on human capital disclosures. Both rulemakings are listed on the SEC's Unified Regulatory Agenda with possible actions in October 2023. However, the timing remains uncertain and these rulemakings may be delayed to 2024 in light of the ongoing controversy and threats of litigation related to ESG. Any proposed and final regulations are expected to result in increased attention and oversight, particularly by House Republicans.
